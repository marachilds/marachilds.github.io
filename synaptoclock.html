User Research, Prototyping

introduction

Summary

A quarter-long research and design process that addressed wandering seniors with Alzheimer's disease.

Context

Intellectual Foundations of Informatics at the University of Washington with Professor Joseph Janes. January–March 2017.

Role

Project Manager, UI Design. Delegated tasks among group members; led team through design process including user research, insight analysis, and iteration; and created all presentation materials.

Tools

Pens and paper, Adobe XD + Photoshop, Squarespace

problem

research

Interviews + Ethnography

We conducted interviews with patients with Alzheimer's disease, as well as their caregivers. Through their interview and a design ethnography, were able to note a number of number of observations that drove design insights. Names from a selected interview and ethnography have been omitted for privacy.

Insights + Market

Although the problem is that Alzheimer's patients are wandering, most solutions available only notify loved ones once the person has already passed a threshold and has left the premise. From our research, it was clear that Alzheimer’s patients who were in between living alone and being in full time care want to maintain control over their lives but need assistance with orienting themselves. We focused on a solution that would prevent wandering in the first place, and one that would be not only cost-effective for caregivers, but lightweight and easy to learn.

personas

We built four personas from the four main categories of stakeholders, as derived from our research.

prototyping

Solution

SynaptoClock is a smart clock that is connected to a companion app for the caregiver, and includes extra information for the person with Alzheimer's. With a light-changing feature, SynaptoClock blends into the background and naturally transitions from blue light in the morning to soft yellow light at night. The hardware remains in concept while the application has a working prototype.

Companion App

Reusing familiar iOS UI elements, the companion app is used only to control and manage connected SynaptoClocks. The final working app demo can be viewed here.

testing

We did get to observe two of our research participants, caregivers, using an earlier version of the application demo on their iPhones—one who was self-described as proficient at using their iPhone, and one who said they could use it at a basic level. While we knew we would not have time to iterate past our final version, we kept the familiar UI elements (such as the toggle) after hearing from both users that recognizing controls was the most important part of learning to use the application.

next steps

In a different context, we would continue testing, create a hardware prototype, and test with both working elements of our solution. While we did not continue iterating after our working app demo version, this project was incredibly valuable for its practice with research, insights, and prototype development with real users.
